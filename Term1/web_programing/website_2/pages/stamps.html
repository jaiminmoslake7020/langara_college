Australia

The Australia 1955 SG 288 First South Australian Postage Stamps Fine Mint is very popular stamp and even considered as one of the oldest Australia stamp. The queen Elizabeth picture in the stamp highlights the South Australian Postage. This stamp is not found commonly on most of the stores, it was sold at a very high cost.

Indonesia

Unfortunately, it is not easy to find stamp dealers in Indonesia. A gallery in the Jakarta’s old town displays various old Indonesian stamps of Netherlands Indies and the Japanese Occupation 1942-1945 priced at between Rp 1 to 5 million.The 1946 $1 Redhead Ducks, Bright Rose Pink shade (RW13a) with rare duck stamp color error was sold for $32,200 during Signature New York Stamps Auction in December last year.

Canada

In March, 1859, Mr. Griffin wrote to the printers in New York and stated that the Postmaster General wished to procure postage stamps expressed in cents instead of pence. On the 16th March, the Department placed an order for 1-cent, 5-cent, 10-cent, 12 ½-cent, and 17-cent denominations of similar designs to those used in the past issues.Portrait of Queen Victoria from a contemporary postage stamp design of Great Britain.

Hong Kong

In 1954, the first QEII definitive design was introduced, including a Wilding profile head. The design was similar to the previous Hong Kong definitives for King George VI, which is nice if you like continuation, but not half as spectacular as some of its successors. So we move on swiftly to the next set, introduced in 1962, based on the famous Annigoni portrait. A massive leap forward as far as originality and beauty is concerned.

Malaysia

The White-rumped Shama (Copsychus malabaricus) stamp is popular stamp in Malaysia. The theme of the stamp is animals(fauna) especially birds. The stamp was issued on 2005-05-14 and size of the stamp is 25 x 30 mm. The face value is 50 Malaysian sen.

New Zealand

The fantail featured in a special pictorial issue of stamps in the 1930s. The fantail or 'piwakawaka' in Maori, is a very sociable New Zealand native. The bird's name derives from its beautiful tail of twelve feathers, which broadly expands as the bird flits about. In the background of the design is one of the best-known flowers of the New Zealand bush, the beautiful clematis, which is particularly appropriate, as both the bird and the flower are usually found near the edge of the forest.
